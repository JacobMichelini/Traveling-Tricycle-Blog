Getting There

After Jacob's dad dropped us off at the Seattle airport, we waited for a few hours near our terminal, got lost, and just figured out how to catch the train to our gate as our plane was boarding at 1:30 AM. We then endured / enjoyed twelve hours of sitting and eating multi-course meals served to us by pretty flight attendants (I tried lung - tasted like badly pickled cabbage). We blearily walked off the plane and were quickly invigorated by the Taipei airport. One waiting room was Hello Kitty themed, complete with Hello Kitty phone booths, ours was flight-history themed: blue everywhere with hot air balloons to sit in. One more three hour flight and we arrived in Bangkok, Thailand.

Customs was a breeze, although we were prepared for the worst. We realized that we really should have brought cash (BofA charges a $10 fee plus a bad conversion rate every time you withdraw money), but what can ya do. So, we set out on our journey. It was hot. With our packs on our backs, we walked four or five hours through the city of Bangkok. Did I (Hillary) mention it was hot? I'm not sure if I've ever sweat that much in my life. Bangkok is sprawling. There seems to be no epicenter. Just high towers, tiny shanty buildings, and then more high towers, and more tiny shanty buildings.

Air Conditioning!

Finally, we found our luxurious $30 a night hotel. We were instantly served sweet juice in tiny martini glasses and led to our room. A clean bed, air conditioning, and rain shower after five hours of walking through the steaming city of bangkok is... quite orgasmic.

Bangkok to Chiang Mai

We spent three days wandering around Bangkok, eating street food and finding nice areas. On the fourth day, we took the 15 hour train to Chiang Mai, a city in northern Thailand (Bangkok is in the south). Chiang Mai is quite touristy, but not in a bad way, and very cheap. We stayed in the 'old-city', a mile-by-mile square that is the epicenter of the city. There are about two massage parlors a block ($4-8 hour long massage. Yeah.), and a Seven-11 every few blocks (glorious air conditioned escapes with delicious melon milk and packaged pork burgers).

Chiang Mai Deets

When you see a person on the street in Chiang Mai, chances are their job is to cater to tourists, or they are a tourist. Sort of unsettling, but it also means that you can meet a bunch of people traveling from all over the world, and it's easy to adventure and eat. Even when the people here are trying to scam you, they do it in the nicest way. People never seem to be angry or annoyed when you don't buy their goods or say no to a scam, which is really quite nice! It's always depressing when somebody's kind... until it becomes clear that you aren't going to give them money.

VIP House; Julie's

We spent the first few days exploring Chiang Mai by foot - food, massage parlours, food, and more massage parlours. The pad thai here is about $1 at cheap places. Although we haven't yet found one better than our own (to our American tongues). We did three nights in a nice hostel called VIP House, for 350 baht a night, air conditioned ($10). Next, we tried Julie's, which is (justifiably) ranked highly online. They didn't have air conditioned rooms, which made sleeping hard, but there was an awesome, colorful common room where all the travelers hung out.

Bike Ride!

After a few days of wandering by foot, we decided to rent some bikes for 50 baht ($1.50), and we traveled eastward. The larger roads are scary; you have to be careful: we were not given helmets and everyone drives haphazardly. It's also very noisy (signalmen blow horribly high pitched whistles consitantly with no apparent rhyme or reason). Once you get out of the epicenter of the city, however, it calms down. We eventually made it to Chiang Mai university and some pretty rural areas with throngs of butterflies and a pack of wild peacocks.
