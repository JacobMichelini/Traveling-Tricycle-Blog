Chiang Mai Trekking

A huge component of the tourism industry here are 'treks'. There are hilariously worded (seriously, has no one heard of google translate?) posters for adventures - elephant riding, rafting, visiting hill-tribes, zip-lining, and hiking - all around the city. Many hostels, including Julie's, will not let you stay more than a few days unless you book a trek through them. We really liked Julie's, and I really wanted to go on a trek, so we decided to take the plunge.

However, we didn't want to stay with hill-tribe people (something about paying people so you can blatantly stare at their 'culture' does not sit well with me), and we did not want to ride elephants (training elephants consists of chaining them up and poking them with sticks for days until their spirits break, and riding elephants is also terrible for their backs). Weirdly, we had to pay a premium not to do these things and just go on a long hike. Ideally, we would have found a wonderful hike and waterfall on our own, but figuring out how to get to a good area with no car and no good online directions is difficult. 1200 baht ($40) per person was a lot, but it turned out to be absolutely worth it.

Grumpypants

Okay, so honestly, I was a bit bitter when we set out. It was super early, I was tired, and I was annoyed that I had convinced Jacob and myself to spend eighty bucks to go on a hike. A freaking hike! What a rip off, and it was all my fault. Sleepily, Jacob and I piled into the covered back of a truck with four other travelers - a couple from Belgium and a couple on their honeymoon from Isreal, and the ride started. My mood slowly lifted as we drove the long drive to wherever we were going. The other hikers were friendly; they were, like us, not so keen on the idea of staring at hill-tribe people for money or effectively encouraging elephant abuse. Also, as we drove on, the landscape became more and more jungly: more and more beautiful. After an hour and a half of driving, I grudgingly admitted to myself that even with a rented car and a lot of interneting, I would not have known how to get to this place.

We had arrived at a notorious 'hill-tribe' village. What this really meant is a neighborhood / town of fairly poor farmers; I'm not sure where 'tribe' comes into it. Local dogs and chickens pattered around but for the most part the place looked deserted of people. We stopped into a small store to buy some water, walked around a few houses (this made me feel intrusive, but we saw no one), and eventually made it to a tiny trail on a hill that led through a field of rice.

High

As we started decending out of the (gorgeous) farming fields and into the forest, the beauty of it all really hit me. I don't know about everyone else, but I used to dream of rainforests as a kid. I remember imagining walking through jungle so lush and green that ... absolutely every space was filled with something alive and different and verdant. And this was it! Holy shit. Jacob and I have discussed this: we literally get a feeling of being high when we see beautiful natural scenes. And friends, family, relatives, conservative folk: I was high as a kite.

Hike

The trail we were on was clearly remote, it was just us six and our guide - I asked our guide about it, and he laughed and described how his boss had bribed a government official in order to get permission to make the trail. Is it bad that this made me feel good about spending so much money?...Tarzan could have been happy here. Thick vines descended from many of the trees, curving and graceful but still strong enough to stand on. It would be awesome to have some of these in your back yard!
